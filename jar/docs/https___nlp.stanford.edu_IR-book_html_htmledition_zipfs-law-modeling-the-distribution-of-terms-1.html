<H2><A NAME="SECTION001012000000000000000"></A>
<A NAME="sec:zipf"></A> <A NAME="p:zipf"></A>
<BR>
Zipf's law: Modeling the distribution of terms
</H2>
We also want to understand
how terms are distributed across documents. This
helps us to characterize the properties of the
algorithms for compressing postings lists in Section <A HREF="postings-file-compression-1.html#sec:postingscompression">5.3</A> .

<P>
A commonly used model of the distribution of terms in a
collection is <A NAME="5958"></A> <I>Zipf's law</I> . It states that, if
<IMG
WIDTH="17" HEIGHT="32" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img243.png"
ALT="$t_1$"> is the
most common term in the collection, <IMG
WIDTH="16" HEIGHT="32" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img244.png"
ALT="$t_2$"> is the next most
common, and so on, then the collection frequency
<IMG
WIDTH="21" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img245.png"
ALT="$\collf_i$"><A NAME="collf-i-notation"></A> of the <IMG
WIDTH="8" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img8.png"
ALT="$i$">th most common
term is proportional to <IMG
WIDTH="26" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img246.png"
ALT="$1/i$">:
<BR>
<DIV ALIGN="CENTER"><A NAME="zipf"></A>
<!-- MATH
\begin{eqnarray}
\collf_i \propto \frac{1}{i}.
\end{eqnarray}
-->
<TABLE ALIGN="CENTER" CELLPADDING="0" WIDTH="100%">
<TR VALIGN="MIDDLE"><TD NOWRAP ALIGN="RIGHT"><IMG
WIDTH="56" HEIGHT="52" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img247.png"
ALT="$\displaystyle \collf_i \propto \frac{1}{i}.$"></TD>
<TD>&nbsp;</TD>
<TD>&nbsp;</TD>
<TD WIDTH=10 ALIGN="RIGHT">
(3)</TD></TR>
</TABLE></DIV>
<BR CLEAR="ALL"><P></P>
So if the most frequent term occurs <IMG
WIDTH="23" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img248.png"
ALT="$\collf_1$"> times, then the
second most frequent term has half as many occurrences, the third
most frequent term a third as many occurrences, and so on. The
intuition is that frequency decreases very rapidly with
rank. Equation <A HREF="#zipf">3</A> is one of the simplest ways of
formalizing such a rapid decrease and it has been found to
be a reasonably good model.

<P>
Equivalently, we can write Zipf's law as <!-- MATH
$\collf_i = c i^k$
-->
<IMG
WIDTH="61" HEIGHT="37" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img249.png"
ALT="$ \collf_i = c i^k$"> or
as <!-- MATH
$\log \collf_i = \log c + k \log i$
-->
<IMG
WIDTH="158" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img250.png"
ALT="$\log \collf_i = \log c + k \log i$"> where <IMG
WIDTH="55" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img251.png"
ALT="$k=-1$"> and <IMG
WIDTH="11" HEIGHT="32" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img252.png"
ALT="$c$"> is
a constant to be defined in Section <A HREF="gamma-codes-1.html#sec:gamma-code">5.3.2</A> . It is therefore a
<A NAME="5968"></A> <I>power law</I> with exponent <IMG
WIDTH="55" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img251.png"
ALT="$k=-1$">. See
Chapter <A HREF="web-search-basics-1.html#ch:webchar">19</A> , page <A HREF="the-web-graph-1.html#p:webpowerlaw">19.2.1</A> , for another
power law, a law characterizing the distribution of links on web
pages.

<P>

<DIV ALIGN="CENTER">

<P><A NAME="fig:zipfnew"></A><A NAME="p:zipfnew"></A></P><IMG
WIDTH="406" HEIGHT="371" ALIGN="BOTTOM" BORDER="0"

ALT="\includegraphics[width=9cm]{art/zipfnew.eps}">
<A NAME="5974"></A>
Zipf's law for Reuters-RCV1.
Frequency is plotted as a function of frequency rank for
the terms in the
collection. The line is the distribution predicted by Zipf's
law (weighted least-squares fit; intercept is 6.95).


</DIV>

<P>
The log-log graph in Figure <A HREF="#fig:zipfnew">5.2</A> plots the
collection frequency of a term as a function of its rank for
Reuters-RCV1. A line with slope -1, corresponding to
the Zipf function <!-- MATH
$\log \collf_i = \log c -\log i$
-->
<IMG
WIDTH="148" HEIGHT="31" ALIGN="MIDDLE" BORDER="0"
SRC="img254.png"
ALT="$\log \collf_i = \log c -\log i$">, is also shown.
The fit of the data to the law is not particularly good, but
good enough to serve as a model for term distributions in
our calculations in Section <A HREF="postings-file-compression-1.html#sec:postingscompression">5.3</A> .
